# Improved Instructions For Viewing Option Researchers

Research date: 2026-06-22

Use this as an addendum to `docs/research-agents/viewing-agent-briefs.md` for any agent proposing Tour de France viewing points.

## Required Verification For Every Viewing Option

Every candidate viewing point must verify or explicitly mark unknown:

- Exact coordinates of the intended standing area, not just the town or climb.
- Official stage ID and stage date.
- Race-road segment, route landmark, or nearest official time-schedule point.
- Whether the point is on the race route, near the start/finish security perimeter, or on a side approach.
- Official route source used: Tour stage page, official map, time schedule, or official GIS/GeoJSON if available.
- Road closure source for the municipality/department/prefecture, including closure start/end if published.
- Parking legality and location. Distinguish official parking, normal public parking, roadside parking, and unknown.
- Transit/shuttle options and whether event-day changes are published.
- Walking or cycling approach after vehicle closure.
- Toilets, food, water, and shade/shelter nearby, with a source or clear "unknown."
- Crowd risk: low/medium/high plus why.
- Exit plan after the riders and race convoy pass.
- Whether the publicity caravan is expected to pass the point; do not assume exact offset unless sourced.
- Safety assessment: standing room off the roadway, barriers, blind corners, narrow road, drop-offs, bridges, tunnels, or vehicle-convoy exposure.
- Open questions that a planner must resolve before using the spot.

## Trustworthy Source Order

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Official Tour de France / ASO pages: route, stage maps, time schedules, classifications, start/finish details.
2. Official embedded map/GIS route data when available.
3. Prefecture, department, municipality, police/gendarmerie, and transport-agency pages for road closures, parking bans, shuttles, and station changes.
4. Official city tourism or event pages for fan zones, toilets, big screens, and visitor logistics.
5. Venue/business websites for cafes, restaurants, toilets, opening hours, and reservations.
6. Local news for closure summaries, crowd expectations, access changes, and practical reports; confirm against official notices when possible.
7. Mapping services for coordinates, terrain, walking distances, and street layout; do not use maps alone to prove access.
8. Cycling media and experienced-fan guides for general viewing strategy, atmosphere, and historical context.
9. Blogs/forums/social posts only as weak supporting context unless they include direct photos, official notice screenshots, or first-hand access details that can be independently checked.

## What To Treat As Uncertain

Mark low confidence unless directly sourced:

- Any statement that a road will remain open, reopen quickly, or allow parking.
- Side-road access near the course.
- Ability to cross the race road after closures.
- Mountain-pass access, summit parking, camper parking, and descent timing.
- Finish-line public access, especially within the final kilometer.
- Toilets and food access once barriers are installed.
- Exact caravan timing at a viewing point.
- Crowd size for iconic climbs, city circuits, starts, and finishes.
- Whether a good-looking map location has legal pedestrian space.
- Whether restaurants/cafes on the route will be open, reachable, or reservation-only.

## Red Flags That Make A Viewing Point Impractical

Reject or downgrade candidates with:

- No sourced access route that avoids closed roads.
- Parking plan that requires using or crossing the race route after closure.
- Standing area on a blind corner, tunnel, bridge, narrow shoulder, steep drop-off, or traffic island.
- Need to stand in the roadway to see the riders.
- No pedestrian fallback if vehicle access is blocked.
- No bathroom/food/water plan for a long wait, especially in mountains.
- Exit route through the same narrow valley or summit road used by everyone else, with no time buffer.
- Unclear final-kilometer security perimeter or VIP/media-only area.
- Reliance on "should be fine" reasoning from maps without official or local confirmation.
- A point that works only if spectators can cross the course shortly before race passage.

## Better Candidate Notes

When proposing a point, include a short planner-facing sentence like:

`Planning value: likely good for slower rider passage on a climb, but access is not usable until official Tourmalet closure and parking notices are published.`

This makes uncertainty visible without burying the useful idea.

## Source Notes For These Instructions

- [Tour de France official site](https://www.letour.fr/en/) - Use as the primary source for official route, stage, map, and time-schedule data.
- [Cycling Weekly caravan report](https://www.cyclingweekly.com/racing/we-have-a-kind-of-power-a-day-on-mont-ventoux-in-the-tour-de-france-publicity-caravan) - Supports treating the caravan as a major timed event before the riders and a meaningful part of the spectator day.
- [Wikipedia: Tour de France advertising caravan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_de_France#Advertising_caravan) - Secondary support for typical caravan timing and scale; do not use as the only timing source for a specific viewing point.
- [Wikipedia: French Tour de France customs/spectators section](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_de_France#Us_et_coutumes) - Secondary support for crowd scale, mountain crowd compression, and safety risk from spectators near riders and convoy vehicles.
- [Wikipedia: 2021 Tour de France Stage 1 crash summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Tour_de_France,_Stage_1_to_Stage_11#Stage_1) - Supports red flags around signs, road encroachment, and spectator-caused crashes.
- [GQ Greg LeMond viewing tips](https://www.gq.com/story/how-to-watch-the-tour-de-france) - Supports explaining why climbs, sprints, fatigue, and race dynamics affect viewing quality.
